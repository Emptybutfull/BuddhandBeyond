Vajrayana Deities, Bodhisattvas, and Buddhas: Legends and Stories

1. Padmasambhava: The Lotus-Born

In the 8th century CE, in the northwestern part of India, a miraculous event occurred. On a lotus flower in the middle of Lake Dhanakosha, a young boy appeared, fully enlightened and with the appearance of an eight-year-old child. This was Padmasambhava, also known as Guru Rinpoche, whose coming had been prophesied by Buddha Shakyamuni.

The king of the land, Indrabhuti, who was childless, discovered the boy and adopted him as his heir. Padmasambhava lived in the palace for a time, but his extraordinary nature soon became apparent. He would often teach profound dharma to the court, and his behavior was unconventional, to say the least.

Realizing that the royal life was not his path, Padmasambhava left the palace to become a monk at Nalanda University. There, he quickly mastered all the Buddhist teachings and became renowned for his wisdom and miraculous powers. However, his unconventional methods often clashed with the more traditional approach of the monastery.

Padmasambhava then traveled throughout India and the Himalayas, practicing intensive meditation in cremation grounds and remote caves. He subdued powerful local deities and spirits, converting them into protectors of the Dharma. His fame grew, and he became known as a great tantric master.

The pivotal moment in Padmasambhava's life came when he was invited to Tibet by King Trisong Detsen. The king was attempting to establish Buddhism in Tibet but faced fierce opposition from local shamanic traditions and spirits. Padmasambhava's task was to subdue these forces and firmly plant the seeds of Buddhism in Tibetan soil.

Upon arriving in Tibet, Padmasambhava faced numerous challenges. He engaged in magical contests with local sorcerers, invariably defeating them and converting many to Buddhism. He tamed the wild spirits of the land, binding them by oath to protect the Dharma. Most famously, he subdued the spirit Pehar, who became a principal protector deity of the Tibetan government.

Padmasambhava's greatest achievement in Tibet was the founding of Samye Monastery, the first Buddhist monastery in the country. He overcame countless obstacles, both natural and supernatural, to complete its construction. The monastery became a beacon of Buddhist learning and practice in Tibet.

Throughout his time in Tibet, Padmasambhava gave countless teachings and empowerments. He had twenty-five principal disciples, including the king and the great translator Vairocana. To each, he gave specific teachings tailored to their capacities and future roles in spreading the Dharma.

Foreseeing troubled times ahead, Padmasambhava concealed thousands of spiritual treasures (terma) throughout Tibet and the Himalayas. These treasures, consisting of texts, ritual objects, and relics, were meant to be discovered by designated treasure revealers (tertons) in future generations when the time was right for their teachings to flourish.

After establishing Buddhism firmly in Tibet, Padmasambhava departed for the southwestern continent of Chamara, where he is said to reside to this day in an immortal body, continuing to work for the benefit of all beings. His parting words to his Tibetan disciples were a promise that he would return to anyone who called upon him with sincere devotion.

The legacy of Padmasambhava in Tibetan Buddhism cannot be overstated. He is revered as the 'Second Buddha' and is credited with establishing the Nyingma school, the oldest of the four major schools of Tibetan Buddhism. His teachings and methods, particularly those of Dzogchen, continue to be practiced and revered to this day.

Padmasambhava's life story, filled with miraculous events and profound spiritual accomplishments, serves as an inspiration to practitioners. It embodies the Vajrayana ideal of transforming all circumstances, no matter how challenging, into the path of awakening. His promise to return to those who call upon him with devotion continues to provide solace and inspiration to countless practitioners around the world.

2. Avalokiteshvara: The Bodhisattva of Compassion

Avalokiteshvara, known as Chenrezig in Tibetan, is revered as the embodiment of the compassion of all Buddhas. His story begins countless eons ago when he made a vow before the Buddha Amitabha: "I will not rest until I have liberated all sentient beings from samsara. May my head split into a thousand pieces if I ever become discouraged."

For many ages, Avalokiteshvara worked tirelessly to help sentient beings. He descended into the various realms - the hell realms, the hungry ghost realm, the animal realm, the human realm, and the god realms - tirelessly guiding beings towards liberation. However, despite his ceaseless efforts, he saw that the number of suffering beings remained vast.

In a moment of despair, he realized that his task seemed endless. At that instant, in accordance with his vow, his head split into eleven pieces. Amitabha Buddha, seeing Avalokiteshvara's plight, immediately came to his aid. He restored Avalokiteshvara, giving him eleven heads to better hear the cries of suffering beings, and a thousand arms to better aid them.

Thus empowered, Avalokiteshvara redoubled his efforts. He developed a particular connection to the land of Tibet. According to legend, Avalokiteshvara looked upon the land of Tibet and perceived it as a realm of darkness. Moved by compassion, he shed a tear for the beings there. As the tear fell to the earth, it transformed into a lotus, from which emerged Green Tara, who became Avalokiteshvara's consort in his mission to liberate beings.

Avalokiteshvara is said to have manifested in Tibet in various forms throughout history. One of the most famous instances is his incarnation as the Tibetan king Songtsen Gampo in the 7th century. As Songtsen Gampo, Avalokiteshvara played a crucial role in establishing Buddhism in Tibet, building temples, and initiating the translation of Buddhist texts into Tibetan.

Another significant manifestation of Avalokiteshvara in Tibetan history is through the lineage of the Dalai Lamas. Each Dalai Lama is considered to be an emanation of Avalokiteshvara, continuing his work of compassion in the world. The current 14th Dalai Lama, Tenzin Gyatso, is thus seen as a living embodiment of Avalokiteshvara's compassionate activity.

The mantra of Avalokiteshvara, "Om Mani Padme Hum," is perhaps the most famous Buddhist mantra. Each syllable is said to purify one of the six realms of existence. The mantra is omnipresent in Tibetan culture, carved on stones, printed on prayer flags, and continually recited by practitioners.

One of the most beloved stories about Avalokiteshvara tells of his creation of the Potala Palace. It's said that Avalokiteshvara once meditated in a cave on Potala Mountain. His meditation was so powerful that the mountain itself transformed into a glorious palace. This celestial palace became the model for the earthly Potala Palace in Lhasa, which served as the residence of the Dalai Lamas.

Avalokiteshvara's compassion is often illustrated through the story of Gelongma Palmo. A princess who had contracted leprosy, Gelongma Palmo was cast out from her kingdom. In her despair, she prayed fervently to Avalokiteshvara. He appeared to her and taught her the practice of Nyung Ne, a fasting and purification practice. Through this practice, she was healed and attained realization.

The vastness of Avalokiteshvara's compassion is symbolized in the Mani Kabum, a text which describes how every part of Avalokiteshvara's body is an instrument to liberate beings. His eyes radiate love that soothes all who see him, his voice teaches the dharma in all languages, and even the pores of his skin emanate countless Buddha realms.

In the Mahayana sutras, Avalokiteshvara is often depicted in dialogue with Buddha Shakyamuni, asking questions on behalf of all beings that elicit profound teachings. In the Heart Sutra, it is Avalokiteshvara who expounds the central Mahayana teaching on emptiness.

The story and iconography of Avalokiteshvara beautifully encapsulate the Mahayana ideal of the bodhisattva - a being who forsakes complete enlightenment to remain in the world, working tirelessly for the benefit of all sentient beings. His tale is one of unfaltering compassion in the face of endless suffering, serving as an inspiration for practitioners to cultivate boundless compassion in their own lives.

3. Vajrayogini: The Diamond Female Yogi

Vajrayogini, also known as Vajravarahi, is one of the most important deities in the Vajrayana Buddhist pantheon. She embodies the wisdom and power of the dakinis, female embodiments of enlightened energy. Her story is deeply intertwined with the development of tantric Buddhism and the lineage of Mahasiddhas in India.

In her most common form, Vajrayogini appears as a vibrant red, sixteen-year-old female, naked except for ornaments, symbolizing her freedom from worldly attachments. She holds a curved knife in her right hand, cutting through ego and delusion, and a skull cup in her left, filled with blood that transforms into wisdom nectar. She stands in a dynamic posture, her left leg raised, ready to dance across samsara and nirvana.

The origins of Vajrayogini trace back to ancient India, where she was likely derived from indigenous goddess worship, later incorporated into the Buddhist tantric system. She is often associated with the Mahasiddha tradition, particularly with the legendary 84 Mahasiddhas who attained enlightenment through unconventional means.

One of the most famous stories involving Vajrayogini is that of the Mahasiddha Naropa. Naropa, a renowned scholar at Nalanda University, encountered Vajrayogini in the form of an old, ugly woman. She asked him if he understood the words of the teachings he was studying. When he proudly said yes, she laughed and said he only understood the words, not the meaning. This encounter led Naropa to leave his prestigious position and search for his guru, Tilopa, beginning his journey as a tantric practitioner.

Another pivotal story in the Vajrayogini tradition is that of Mahasiddha Krishnacharya. It's said that Krishnacharya was meditating in a charnel ground when Vajrayogini appeared to him. She led him to her pure land, Khechara, where he received profound teachings and empowerments. Upon returning to the human realm, Krishnacharya composed the influential "Yogini Tantra," which became a primary source for Vajrayogini practice.

The practice of Vajrayogini is particularly emphasized in the Sakya and Gelug schools of Tibetan Buddhism. In the Sakya tradition, there's a lineage of teachings known as the "Thirteen Golden Dharmas," of which Vajrayogini is considered the most important. The 11th century Indian master Naropa received these teachings directly from Vajrayogini and passed them to his Tibetan disciple Marpa, who in turn transmitted them to the famous yogi Milarepa.

In the Gelug school, the practice of Vajrayogini is often undertaken after completing the preliminary practices (ngöndro) and receiving the necessary empowerments. It's said that the great Je Tsongkhapa, founder of the Gelug school, had a special connection with Vajrayogini. According to tradition, when Tsongkhapa was composing his masterwork, the Lamrim Chenmo, he received direct inspiration from Manjushri and Vajrayogini.

One of the unique aspects of Vajrayogini practice is the promise of attaining enlightenment in one lifetime or at least being reborn in her pure land, Khechara. This pure land is described as a place where the conditions for attaining enlightenment are perfect. It's said to be located "above the crown of the head," symbolizing its transcendent nature.

There are numerous stories of practitioners who have been led to Khechara by Vajrayogini. One famous account tells of the Tibetan yogi Rechungpa, disciple of Milarepa. Near the end of his life, Rechungpa manifested illness as a final teaching to his students. As he lay dying, his body began to shrink until it was the size of a small child. Then, in front of his astonished disciples, his body rose into the air and disappeared in a flash of rainbow light, ascending to Khechara.

Vajrayogini is also associated with the practice of gtum-mo, or inner heat yoga. This advanced practice involves visualizing Vajrayogini in the central channel of the subtle body, igniting the fires of wisdom that burn away all karmic obscurations. Masters of this practice are said to be able to survive in freezing conditions wearing nothing but a thin cotton robe, demonstrating their control over the inner energies.

In the higher tantric practices, Vajrayogini is often practiced in union with Chakrasamvara, representing the union of wisdom and compassion, feminine and masculine energies. This practice is considered one of the most direct paths to enlightenment in the Vajrayana tradition.

The iconography and practice of Vajrayogini embody many key principles of Vajrayana Buddhism. Her nakedness represents the clear, unobscured nature of mind. Her red color symbolizes the passionate energy of compassion that she employs to liberate beings. The skull cup and curved knife represent the inseparability of bliss and emptiness, the ultimate realization in tantric practice.
